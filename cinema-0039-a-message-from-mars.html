A Message From Mars

A Message From Mars, first British science fiction feature film, has been restored by the BFI National Archive. The BFI and the BBC also commissioned a new score, created by Matthew Herbert and New Radiophonic Workshop, to accompany the silent movie. The film’s plot, echoing A Christmas Carol, concerns a Martian exiled to Earth with the mission to change the heart of a selfish man.

The movie, directed by J. Wallett Waller, was first shown in film theatres over 100 years ago in 1913, and is based on a highly popular stage play by the American playwright Richard Ganthony, which saw many revivals in Britain over 30 years. A Message From Mars features the first on-screen representation of Martians (as futuristically clad members of the Martian court) by a British film-maker. Instant space travel, mind control, transference, and the use of a far-seeing crystal ball, also all feature in this landmark film. Restoration specialists at the BFI National Archive, in collaboration with Dragon Digital in Wales, spent over six months painstakingly assembling the fuller, 59 minutes long version, from two shorter copies and a tinted print held by the Museum of Modern Art, as well as restoring the original tinting and toning of the film.

Synopsis: Horace Parker (Charles Hawtrey) is a wealthy, extremely selfish and self-centred man. He is not only a miser, but also expects his family and friends (and everyone else around him) to conduct their lives according to his personal convenience. Parker gets engaged to Minnie Templer (Crissie Bell), but it doesn’t take too long before Minnie sees Parker's true face and is on the brink of calling off the engagement. However, on Christmas Eve, a messenger from Mars comes to Earth to show Parker the error of his ways. Parker and his fiancee become invisible and, unseen by others, eavesdrop on all the terrible – but true – things Parker's family and friends are saying about him.
